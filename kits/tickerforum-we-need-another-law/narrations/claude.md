The records describe conduct that is, on its face, **already illegal under binding law**.
Sherman Act §2 (15 USC 2) makes it a felony to monopolize or attempt to monopolize, carrying
penalties up to $100M for a corporation, $1M for a person, or ten years in prison; 8 USC 1324
criminalizes harboring, transporting, or employing unauthorized aliens with penalties ranging
from one to twenty years. Both are marked WARN — binding law paired with near-zero criminal
enforcement. The enforcement record bears that out: the government brought 100-plus criminal §2
cases in the Act's first eighty years, then none between 1977 and 2022, and the two recent cases
ended in probation rather than decade sentences; corporate-officer imprisonment under §1324 is
likewise rare. So the claim that this conduct is *already covered by law* is well supported by
the records.

What the records do **not** support is the further step from "already illegal" to "successfully
prosecuted" — those are different facts. A near-zero criminal count is a data point, not proof
that crimes went unpunished: it is also consistent with prosecutors routing monopoly cases
through civil enforcement and with the higher proof burden criminal charges carry. Against that
backdrop, three measures sit at WATCH — AICOA (S.4746), the Open App Markets Act (S.2153), and
the AMERICA Act (S.1060) — pending bills, not yet law, proposed to address conduct the existing
felony arguably already reaches. The sharpest WARN is the CPT/CMS mandate, where the government
itself requires providers to license the AMA's copyrighted billing codes to bill federal
programs. None of this establishes that any named party has committed a crime; that is a
question for a court, not a corpus.
