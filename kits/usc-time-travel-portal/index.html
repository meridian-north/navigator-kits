Why this withstands scrutiny — three points before you cite

The sources are the sources. Built from official USC editions published by the Office of Law Revision Counsel and the U.S. Statutes at Large. The AI does not generate law; it indexes law that already existed and makes it queryable across five decades. Every record links to its authoritative origin.
No model in the result path. What you read is text extracted from the official govinfo.gov PDF for that edition year. No summary was generated; no paraphrase was inserted. This is what keeps the tool clear of the Rule 11 / Rule 3.3 hallucinated-citation exposure that has already drawn sanctions elsewhere.
The disagreement surface is the finding. Each section is scored independently by two models from different families — a 2-billion-parameter deterministic model and a 72-billion-parameter cross-check. Where they agree, the verdict is corroborated. Where they disagree, the section is flagged read twice — that is exactly where human legal judgment matters most. The tool maps the edge of certainty; it does not pretend the edge isn't there.

Caveat travels with every result: snapshot_year_is_not_enactment_date. Use the official USC for filing; use this as your compass.

A reproducible record. The premise of this corpus is that any output can be re-run by anyone — opposing counsel included — against the same pinned corpus version and return the byte-identical result, the way a certified copy is the same as the original.

Posture. This system surfaces; it does not conclude. It reports what the primary sources say and where certainty ends. Leads, not verdicts. Only humans conclude.

Verify a hash

Every corpus release ships a MANIFEST_SHA256.txt. Paste the SHA-256 a record claims and the one you computed from the source PDF; the tool reports match or mismatch. It performs the comparison only — it makes no representation about the file you fetched.
