The system removes problem vaccines — when it has the data

When post-market surveillance caught a real, identifiable signal, the system acted: suspended, replaced, or de-recommended the product. These are sharp, identifiable signals. The subtler, diffuse, product- or lot-specific questions are exactly what a denominated, linked system would let anyone check. Pro-safety, not anti-vaccine — and an argument for opening the data, not closing it.

2 · Why our null is honest about what it can't see (the anti-placebo limit)

Our schedule check compares each vaccination age to its adjacent days. That catches a sharp post-shot spike — but on the dense infant schedule the neighbor days aren't a true placebo, so a diffuse effect lifts them too and gets subtracted out. Left: a sharp spike stands above its neighbors (caught — this is how rotavirus/intussusception flags). Right: a diffuse rise hides inside the comparison (missed). Beating this needs a true unvaccinated baseline — the withheld linked data.

3 · Every count is a fraction missing its bottom number

A widely-shared chart compares COVID-vaccine death reports in 2021 (1,932) with all other vaccines' reports over 2001–2021 (404). But a count is only the top of a fraction — and the bottom number (how many doses, and how hard people were looking) is missing for both bars, and for every side of the debate.

4 · What the denominator turns a count into

Add the missing pieces and a scary count becomes something you can actually judge — not by picking a side, but by computing one:

1,932 reports ÷ doses given → a report rate (better — but reports still scale with attention) vs deaths expected by background → observed vs expected — now judgeable

Done this way the engine flags real signals and clears coincidences. Worked example: rotavirus→intussusception came out 6 observed vs 0.72 expected (O/E 8.3) — a real signal, and that vaccine was withdrawn in 1999. The same machine returns an honest null where there's nothing. A per-dose report rate alone still isn't a risk rate — only the comparison to expected is, and that needs the linked, denominated data.

5 · When the signal took too long — and what fixes it

Two histories, one lesson. A passive, count-only system is slow and can't size a signal; a denominated, linked one catches and quantifies it. This is not "drugs bad" or "vaccines bad" — it's about how fast the system can protect people.

Vioxx — the slow way (a drug)

A heart-attack signal appeared in a trial in 2000 — and was explained away. A placebo trial confirmed it and the drug was withdrawn in 2004. An FDA scientist later estimated ~88,000–140,000 excess serious cardiac events over its market life. The ~4-year gap is why Congress (FDAAA 2007) created the FDA Sentinel active-surveillance system in 2008 — so the next signal wouldn't take four years.

Pandemrix — the fast way (a vaccine)

In 2010, Sweden and Finland saw a cluster of childhood narcolepsy after the H1N1 vaccine. Because the Nordics have linked health registries, the signal was confirmed in months — a roughly 12–13× increased risk in vaccinated children. The denominated, linked model worked exactly as it should.

Vioxx is why the US built denominated active surveillance; Pandemrix shows it works. The ask is simply to make that capability public and reproducible, so any citizen — not just a few researchers — can run the check.
